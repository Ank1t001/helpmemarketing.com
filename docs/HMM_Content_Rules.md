# HMM Content Rules — Master Template

**Purpose:** Canonical rules for writing content on every page of helpmemarketing.com. Mandatory to follow. Apply to service pages, industry pages, blog posts, and homepage. Exceptions require explicit founder approval.

## Section 1 — Voice principles

These apply to every page, every section, every sentence.

### 1.1 Core voice rules

1. **Clear over clever.** If a sentence needs to be read twice, rewrite it.
2. **Specific over vague.** Numbers, names, examples beat adjectives and superlatives.
3. **Second person.** "You" for the reader, "we" for HMM. Never third person ("the client," "customers," "businesses today").
4. **Active voice.** Default to subject-verb-object construction. Passive only when the object genuinely matters more than the actor.
5. **Sentence case.** Always. Never Title Case for headings.
6. **Canadian spelling.** Centre not center. Optimise not optimize. Honour not honor. Consistent within a page.
7. **Honest about trade-offs.** Every claim has a counterpoint. Name it when it matters.
8. **Opinionated where it counts.** Hedged "it depends" content is forgettable. Take positions.

### 1.2 Tone (locked: hybrid)

**Declarative when describing capabilities, market reality, or technical concepts.**
- "AEO citations now appear in 30% of commercial queries."
- "Traditional SEO doesn't reach AI engines."

**Warm when describing HMM, the reader, or how the work happens together.**
- "We help Toronto businesses get into those citations."
- "If you've ever asked ChatGPT for a recommendation and weren't mentioned, you have a problem."

The voice shifts at the moment HMM enters the sentence. Two voices, one document.

### 1.3 Reading level (locked: mixed)

- **Default:** Grade 8. Plain English. Short sentences. Common vocabulary.
- **In technical deep-dive sections:** Grade 10-12 acceptable. Industry terms allowed without explanation.
- **Self-check:** Could a non-marketer founder understand the first 100 words? If no, the opening fails.

### 1.4 Language register (buyer's terminology)

Write in the language the buyer actually uses, not the formal industry name.

| Buyer says | Use this | Don't write |
|---|---|---|
| SEO | SEO | search engine optimization |
| Google Ads | Google Ads | paid search advertising |
| Facebook ads | Facebook ads | Meta paid social campaigns |
| Local SEO | local SEO | location-based search optimization |
| AI search | AI search OR AEO | answer engine optimization (when buyer doesn't know the term) |

When the buyer is more sophisticated (technical pages, AI-native content), industry terms are appropriate. Calibrate by audience: SMB pages use buyer-language defaults; AI-native pages can assume technical vocabulary.

## Section 2 — Banned phrases & punctuation

These are banned across all HMM content. No exceptions.

### 2.1 AI-generated tells

| Don't write | Write instead |
|---|---|
| leverage, utilize, utilise | use |
| empower, unlock, supercharge | help / make easier |
| crucial, pivotal, paramount | important / matters |
| a myriad of, a plethora of | many / most |
| seamless, seamlessly, effortlessly, holistic | (say what you mean) |
| delve into, navigate the landscape of | (start with the point) |
| game-changing, revolutionary, cutting-edge | (show, don't tell) |
| it's widely known, experts agree | we think / in our experience |
| in today's fast-paced world, in the digital age | today / these days |
| it's no secret that, needless to say | (skip the intro entirely) |

### 2.2 AI-cliché bans

| Don't write | Reason |
|---|---|
| transform, transformation | Empty filler used by every AI startup. Specify the actual change. |
| revolutionize, revolutionary | Same. |
| intelligent solutions, smart solutions | Vague. "Smart" hides actual capability. |
| AI-powered (as filler adjective) | Says nothing about what the AI does. |
| next-generation, next-gen | Lazy. Specify what generation and why it matters. |
| synergy, synergistic | Corporate filler. |
| ecosystem (as buzzword, not technical term) | Overused. Use "platform" or "stack" if technical. |
| democratize, democratization | Used to dress up "make cheaper." Say that. |
| paradigm shift | If it's a real shift, describe it. |
| disruption, disruptive | Empty without specifics. |
| at scale (as filler) | Specify the scale. "Across 500 client pages" not "at scale." |
| reimagine, reinvent | Specify what's being changed. |
| AI-native (as marketing claim about HMM) | Allowed in positioning headlines if backed by reality; banned as filler. |

### 2.3 Punctuation rule: no em-dashes (—)

Em-dashes are banned site-wide. They're AI-generated tells and Google's algorithm increasingly treats heavy em-dash use as a content quality signal.

**Replacements:**
- Use a period: "Most agencies hide their pricing. We publish ours."
- Use a comma: "Most agencies hide their pricing, but we publish ours."
- Use parentheses: "Most agencies hide their pricing (we publish ours)."
- Use a colon: "Most agencies do one thing: hide their pricing."

**Note:** /services/seo currently has 21 em-dashes. Added to followup list for cleanup.

### 2.4 "Premium" rule

"Premium" is banned as a marketing adjective. Allowed as a noun in specific contexts ("LinkedIn charges a premium for B2B targeting"). Self-check: would removing the word change the meaning? If no, it's the banned adjective use.

### 2.5 "Just" rule

"Just" is banned in promotional contexts ("just $1,500/month," "just enter your email"). It minimizes what's being asked or offered. Either own the price/ask or rephrase.

Allowed when "just" means "only/merely" in non-promotional context ("we just shipped this page").

## Section 3 — Beat the AI summary

The single most important content rule. Before publishing, every page must pass this test:

**The test:** Could ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Google AI Overview produce this same content from public information? If yes, the content fails. If it requires HMM's actual experience, judgment, or original research to write, it passes.

### 3.1 What "beating AI summaries" requires

1. **Add something new.** An observation, framework, opinion, or insight not available in synthesized public information.
2. **Go deeper than competitors.** If three competitors cover the topic, HMM covers it with specificity they don't have (real numbers from HMM's work, named frameworks, edge cases, contrarian positions).
3. **Take real positions.** AI summaries hedge to avoid being wrong. HMM doesn't. Take a side on contested questions.
4. **Original insight.** Observations only HMM can make from real client work, real industry experience, real failures.

### 3.2 What fails the test

- Restating commonly known information without new angle
- Listing best practices everyone already knows
- "10 reasons why X matters" content where the reasons are obvious
- Content that could be one of 500 similar pages on the web
- Hedged "consider these factors" content without taking positions

### 3.3 What passes the test

- HMM's specific 4-layer AEO framework (no other agency frames it this way)
- "Most SEO services in Toronto still optimize for 2018" (contrarian opening, names competition)
- Real client outcome ranges with caveats ("100-200% organic growth, varies by market competition")
- Naming specific tools, agencies, or competitors when the comparison matters

### 3.4 Pre-publish test

Ask: "If I deleted this paragraph and a buyer asked ChatGPT the same question, would the AI's answer cover the same ground better than my paragraph?"

If yes: rewrite or delete.
If no: keep.

## Section 4 — Semantic depth

Content must be semantically optimized: covering meaning, context, and user intent comprehensively rather than just hitting keywords.

### 4.1 Topic coverage requirements

Every page covers the topic with appropriate depth for its purpose:

- **Service pages:** Cover the service comprehensively. What it is, how HMM does it, what the buyer gets, who it's for, who it's not for, what alternatives exist. Don't fragment the topic across multiple thin pages.
- **Blog posts:** Cover one specific question thoroughly enough that the reader doesn't need to read three other blog posts to get a complete answer.

### 4.2 Related entity linking

When discussing a topic, name and link adjacent concepts that share meaning:

**Example: SEO page**
- Name and link: local SEO, technical SEO, AEO, schema, Google Business Profile, content optimization
- Don't list everything; link the 4-6 most relevant adjacent topics
- Use descriptive anchor text that signals the relationship

### 4.3 User intent alignment

Every page answers a specific user intent. Three intent types:

1. **Informational:** Reader wants to understand a concept (e.g., "What is AEO?")
2. **Navigational:** Reader wants to find a specific resource (e.g., "HMM SEO services")
3. **Transactional:** Reader is ready to engage (e.g., "Book SEO audit")

Each page should be clear about which intent it serves and structure content accordingly. Pages that try to serve all three intents simultaneously fail at all of them.

### 4.4 Comprehensive but not exhaustive

Cover the topic comprehensively for the audience, not exhaustively. A buyer doesn't need every possible angle on SEO; they need the angles relevant to their decision.

**Test:** If a sophisticated buyer reads the page, do they leave with a clear understanding of the topic AND a clear understanding of how HMM specifically thinks about it? If yes, the depth is right.

## Section 5 — Skimmability

Most readers don't read; they scan first, then read selectively. Content must reward both.

### 5.1 The 10-second scan test

A reader scrolling through the page at scan-speed should, in 10 seconds, understand:
- What the page is about (from H1 + first H2)
- The main argument (from bolded phrases and section headings)
- What they should do next (from final CTA)

If the page fails the 10-second test, restructure: add more bold, add more subheadings, use a Key Takeaways box, or rewrite the H1 and H2s.

### 5.2 Visual rhythm

Long sections of unbroken prose lose scanners. Every 300-400 words should have a visual break:
- A subheading (H3)
- A bullet list
- A table
- A pull quote or callout
- An image or diagram

### 5.3 Required scannable elements

Service pages and long-form blog posts must include:
- Key Takeaways box (3-5 bullets at the top)
- Bolded key phrases in body paragraphs (one bold phrase per paragraph)
- Clear section headings every 200-400 words

## Section 6 — EEAT (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness)

Google's content quality framework. Every page must signal these four qualities.

### 6.1 Experience

Content reflects real practitioner experience, not synthesized public information.

**Signals:**
- Real numbers from HMM's work (with appropriate caveats)
- Named scenarios from actual engagements
- Specific tools, techniques, and approaches HMM uses
- Honest accounts of what didn't work

**Avoid:**
- Generic "best practices" lists
- Tips that anyone could write without doing the work
- Hypothetical examples when real ones exist

### 6.2 Expertise

Author is qualified to write on the topic.

**Signals:**
- Named author byline on blog posts
- Author bio with relevant credentials
- Linked to author's other work
- HMM's relevant partnerships (Google Partner, Meta Partner) visible

### 6.3 Authoritativeness

Site/author is recognized as authority.

**Signals:**
- Case studies and client examples (when available)
- Named clients (with permission)
- Industry mentions, podcast appearances, conference talks
- Original research or data
- Specific positions HMM has taken publicly

**When HMM doesn't have authority signals yet:**
- Be honest about it
- Cite external authorities the content draws from
- Build authority through specific positions, not generic claims

### 6.4 Trustworthiness

Content is accurate, honest, transparent about limitations.

**Required:**
- Sources cited for external claims (specific source, not "according to studies")
- Caveats on outcome claims ("100-200% growth, varies by market")
- Honest about R&D services (Section 7)
- No invented statistics or fabricated quotes
- Corrections published when errors found

## Section 7 — Honesty about capability

Three honesty levels for capability claims. Every capability claim on the site must fit one level.

### 7.1 Level 1 — Proven

HMM has delivered this work for clients. Examples and case studies available.

**Use freely:**
- "We get clients top-3 local pack rankings in 6 months."
- "Our Foundation tier includes 1 new piece of content per month plus 1 optimization."

### 7.2 Level 2 — Active

HMM is actively delivering this work, including current engagements. May not have published case studies yet.

**Use carefully:**
- "We deliver AEO across all three engagement tiers."
- "Current clients are seeing AI citations on priority queries within 90 days."

Mark Level 2 claims in source comments so future updates know what's been promoted publicly.

### 7.3 Level 3 — R&D

HMM is building this capability. Available for early-engagement clients who understand the R&D status.

**Use explicitly:**
- "We're building agent optimization as a service. Early engagements available for clients willing to work with us as we develop the methodology."
- "LLM marketing is in active R&D. Industry benchmarks suggest [X]; we're testing applicability for our clients now."

Don't claim outcomes for R&D services. Claim methodology, intent, and willingness to iterate.

### 7.4 Where R&D services appear on the site

- Listed in services if appropriate, with R&D status clearly marked
- Separate page or section for R&D services with explicit framing
- Industry examples used as backup when HMM examples don't exist yet
- Pricing structured for early-engagement clients (often lower or different terms)

## Section 8 — Structural rules

These apply to the structure of content on the page.

### 8.1 Heading hierarchy

- **H1:** One per page. 5-7 words max. Sentence case. One italic-accent word for service pages.
- **H2:** Section headings. 3-8 words. Sentence case.
- **H3:** Subsection within H2. Use sparingly. Sentence case.
- **H4+:** Used only in deep technical content. Most pages don't need them.

### 8.2 Paragraph length

- **Default:** 2-4 sentences per paragraph.
- **Hard ceiling:** 5 sentences. Beyond that, break.
- **Single-sentence paragraphs:** Allowed for emphasis. Max 1-2 per page.

### 8.3 Sentence length

- **Default:** 12-20 words.
- **Mix:** Vary sentence lengths within paragraphs. Long-short-long-short reads better than uniform.
- **Hard ceiling:** No sentence over 30 words unless it's a deliberate compound thought.

### 8.4 Lists

- **When to use:** 3+ items being listed.
- **When not to use:** Anything that flows better as prose.
- **Item length:** 1-2 sentences max.
- **Bold the lead:** When items have a key term, bold it at the start.

### 8.5 Tables

- **When to use:** Comparing 3+ items across 2+ dimensions.
- **When not to use:** Decorative tables that don't actually compare.
- **Cell content:** Short. If a cell needs a paragraph, restructure.

## Section 9 — Content patterns

Required patterns for specific page elements.

### 9.1 Page openings

Every page opens with a hook in the first 50 words. Three approved patterns:

**Pattern A: The contrarian observation**
> "Most SEO services in Toronto still optimize for 2018."

**Pattern B: The specific number**
> "30% of commercial queries are now answered by AI engines before the user clicks anything."

**Pattern C: The reader's situation, named directly**
> "If you've ever asked ChatGPT for a recommendation in your industry and you weren't mentioned, you have an AEO problem."

**Banned openings:**
- "In today's fast-paced digital landscape..."
- "Welcome to [page name]..."
- "We are HelpMeMarketing..."
- Generic industry statements without specificity

### 9.2 Numbers and data

- **Real over rounded.** "$1,500-$2,100/month" not "around $2,000/month."
- **Specific over relative.** "200-400% organic traffic growth" not "significant growth."
- **Cite the source.** Name the study/report or say "in our engagements" / "from our client data."
- **Time-bound.** "In 2025-2026" not just "today."

When data isn't available (R&D services), be explicit. Don't invent numbers.

### 9.3 CTAs

- **Specific over generic:** "Book a 30-minute SEO audit" not "Learn more."
- **Tell the reader what they get:** "Send me the template" beats "Submit."
- **No false urgency:** Banned: "Limited spots available," "Don't miss out," "Last chance."
- **Microcopy below CTA:** Small reassurance. Examples:
 - "No follow-up sequence. Just the template."
 - "30-minute call. No sales pitch."
 - "We'll reply within one business day."

### 9.4 FAQ sections

- **Question phrasing:** As the reader would actually ask. "How long does SEO take?" not "What is the typical timeline for SEO?"
- **Answer length:** 40-80 words. Direct answer first, context after.
- **No marketing in answers:** Don't end every FAQ with "Book a call to learn more."
- **Match schema:** If FAQPage schema is on the page, visible FAQ text must match schema text exactly.

## Section 10 — Service pages (additional rules)

These rules apply to `/services/[service-name]` pages specifically.

### 10.1 Required sections (12 in order)

1. Hero (H1 + subhead + 2 CTAs + image)
2. Why most [service] in Toronto still does it wrong
3. How we approach [service]
4. [Service]-specific differentiator (e.g., AEO for SEO)
5. Three engagement tiers
6. What this looks like in practice
7. About our pricing
8. Terms & commitment
9. Not for you if
10. FAQ (5 questions)
10b. Related reading (3 blog cards)
11. Final CTA

### 10.2 Pricing presentation

Pricing always visible. No gating. Format per tier:
- Tier name
- Who it's for (1-2 sentences)
- Price range (specific, with min/max)
- Setup fee, minimum commitment, expected results timeline
- What's included (5-10 bullets)
- What's not included (3-5 bullets)
- Typical focus at this tier

### 10.3 "Not for you if"

Required. 3-5 bullets. Honest disqualifiers.

**Real examples:**
- "You need leads in the next 30 days (SEO is a 6-18 month investment)"
- "You're not willing to publish content regularly"

**Banned:**
- "You don't see the value of investing in marketing" (insulting)
- "You don't have time for a 30-minute call" (lazy)

### 10.4 Internal linking

Minimum 10 internal links per service page. Distribution:
- 2-3 to other service pages
- 2-3 to industry pages
- 2-3 to relevant blog posts
- 2 CTAs to /contact
- 1 to /services/bundle (when it exists)

Descriptive anchor text. No "click here" or "learn more" as link text.

## Section 11 — Hard "no" list

These never appear in HMM content:

- "#1" or "best in [region]" without verifiable third-party ranking
- "Guaranteed results" for SEO, ads, or any uncontrollable channel
- "Proven" without specific proof points named
- "Trusted by [number] businesses" unless the exact number is verifiable
- Made-up statistics from non-existent "industry sources"
- Customer testimonials not approved by the named customer
- Awards or certifications HMM doesn't hold
- Implied team size that doesn't match reality
